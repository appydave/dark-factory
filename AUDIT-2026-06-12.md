## The one-paragraph truth

The factory is **less broken and more contradictory** than it feels. The hardest part — the atomic-claim dispatch loop — **genuinely works** (`claim-next.sh`, reaper proven unattended 2026-06-07, 35 jobs through `done/`). The north star **exists, is written down, and is good** (`docs/north-star.md`). What's actually wrong is three structural things, none of which is "needs better AI": (1) **the word "Watchtower" means three different things**, and the docs describing them contradict each other and reality — that's your "spaghetti"; (2) **the system is single-writer by design but nobody enforces it**, so a second session doesn't get blocked, it silently corrupts shared files — that's why two sessions "always conflict"; (3) **no loop is closed end-to-end** — every sentinel collects but nothing delivers or consumes, so "self-managing" can't happen yet because the wires between the apps were never run. Fix those three by hand and the factory can start orchestrating. Detail below.

## 4. Parallelism blockers — why two sessions conflict (concrete)

Your instinct is correct and the reason is structural, not flaky. **The factory is single-writer by design, and nothing enforces it** — so a second session isn't refused, it just collides.

**What actually protects concurrency today:** exactly one thing — the `rename(2)` atomic claim, verified directly:
```
# experiments/watchtower-engine/bin/claim-next.sh:24-31
for f in $(ls -1 "$QUEUE" | sort); do
  if mv "$QUEUE/$f" "$RUNNING/$f" 2>/dev/null; then   # the mutex: one mv wins, loser gets ENOENT
    echo "$RUNNING/$f"; exit 0
  fi
done
```
This is real, correct, and stress-tested (200×8, all pass). **But it only protects the act of claiming a job ticket.** It protects nothing else.

**Concrete conflict points when two sessions run at once:**
1. **Handback collision** — `reports/<queue_id>.json` and run records have **no write-lock**. Two agents finishing work on the same `queue_id` both write the same path, last-write-wins. The failure register already shows handback is fragile (`F-20260608-07`: "Swagger skipped reports/ handback — 4th instance").
2. **Two Marshalls re-dispatch** — there is **no lease/heartbeat** above the queue. Two top-level Marshall sessions both polling the queue can each claim *different* tickets fine, but they share no view of "who's already dispatching what," and both will write to the same `registry/`, `build-state.md`, failure register, and `backlog/`. The mutex gives **correctness on claim, never coordination on state.**
3. **Shared mutable singletons** — `MEMORY.md`, `docs/watchtower/build-state.md`, `failures/register.jsonl`, `backlog/` are written by *every* session with no claim discipline. Two sessions editing these is a silent merge corruption, not a blocked write.
4. **The design admits it** — `build-state.md:63`: *"One watcher, never N. Competing self-watchers thunder-herd; the mutex gives correctness, never fairness."* The architecture's answer to "two sessions" is **"don't"** — N=1 Marshall is the safety model. But that rule lives in a doc, not in a guard, so the system never stops you.

**The fix shape (for later, not now):** the `rename(2)` pattern that already works for job claims is exactly the primitive you'd extend to shared-state writes (claim-by-rename a lock file, or per-session append-only files merged later). The pattern is proven; it's just only applied in one place. Until then, **two sessions are safe only if they touch disjoint files** — which the current layout doesn't guarantee.

## Decision Note — Parallelism & Watchtower (2026-06-12, after review with David)

*This section records what we concluded after walking through the audit together. The sections above are the as-found snapshot; this is the agreed direction. David drove most of this — the audit's original framing was too conservative and is corrected here.*

### Reframe: there are two kinds of parallelism, and the old rule only forbids one

The doc rule **"one watcher, never N"** (`build-state.md:63`) has been mis-read as "no parallelism." It only forbids **N consumers racing on ONE job queue** (one fast session hogs it; the mutex gives correctness, not fairness). It says **nothing** against the thing David actually wants:

- **Hard parallelism (deferred):** two Marshalls coordinating over the *same* body of work (same epic, same files). Needs real distributed locking. Not the near-term goal.
- **Easy parallelism (the goal):** an idea lands → fire a second Marshall on a *different* lane → it doesn't touch the first. Two **independent** lanes. This needs almost no coordination — it's mostly an **identity** problem, not a locking problem.

David's use case is the easy kind. It's been blocked for no good architectural reason.

### The keystone: identity

Root cause of "two sessions always conflict": **Marshalls and Swaggers have no stable IDs.** Two Marshalls both write `running/`, `registry/`, etc., and can't tell each other apart. Give every Marshall/Swagger a stable ID and three things unlock at once:

1. **Multiple Marshalls** become possible — each owns `running/<marshall-id>/`, its own registry slice. No collision.
2. **An existing bug fixes itself** — `dispatch-swagger.sh`'s known limitation (window↔job binding only correct for *serial* dispatch) is an identity bug; IDs fix it for free.
3. **Switchboard gets something real to track** — "Marshall-A live, owns lane X; Marshall-B live, owns lane Y."

Already-true and staying: **one Marshall runs many Swaggers** (existing, correct). The new capability is **many Marshalls**, gated only by identity.

### The agreed sequence (interim → target)

| Step | What | Cost | Gives you |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0. Safety latch (interim)** | Guard that refuses a 2nd Marshall in the *same* engine-state dir until IDs land | tiny | Stops silent corruption *today*; not the strategy, just a stopgap |
| **1. Identity** | Stable unique IDs for Marshall + Swagger | small | The keystone; also fixes the reaper/dispatch serial-binding bug |
| **2. State-partition by ID** | `running/<id>/`, registry-by-id, per-session run records | small | **Multiple Marshalls on one machine — the easy parallelism — with NO Switchboard yet** |
| **3. Worktrees for build jobs** | Each code-building Swagger gets its own git worktree (tooling already supports `isolation: worktree`) | ~free | Filesystem isolation so two builds never edit the same files |
| **4. Switchboard as live registry** | Central state plane: who's live, what each owns, cross-Marshall awareness | the real project | **True multi-writer** — Marshalls aware of each other, not just non-colliding |

**Parallelism is on-machine, not only multi-machine** (David's correction — the audit wrongly implied otherwise). Steps 1–3 deliver one-box multi-Marshall. Step 4 is the graduation to coordinated multi-writer.

**Where shared state belongs:** in a **service** (Switchboard), not in locks scattered across the floor's files. The floor stays a stateless client; "2nd orchestrator" = N thin clients on 1 state plane. (This is the `constellation-first-placement` principle — applied here, finally.)

**Caution (the one place to not rush):** do **not** build the Step-4 Switchboard registry first. Prove identity + partition (Steps 1–2) with two real Marshalls on two real lanes before wiring the coordinator. The subtle bugs live in the coordination layer; earn your way there. Hard parallelism (two Marshalls, one epic) stays deferred.

### Watchtower — resolved (David's call, better than the audit's)

- **`apps/watchtower` is the real, durable home.** It is where a proper Watchtower gets built — rebuilt on the **AppySentinel** (or **AppyStack**) template once the design is de-risked.
- **`experiments/watchtower-engine` is the proof-of-concept / spike** — a *disposable* proving ground for the mechanics (atomic claim, reaper, board), kept only until "all the bullshit is figured out."
- **Then:** graduate the proven mechanics out of `experiments/` into `apps/watchtower` as a real app.

This supersedes the audit's "the engine *is* Watchtower" recommendation — that would have enshrined a spike as production. Keeping experiment-as-disposable / app-as-durable is the correct build discipline. The name collision (Drift #1) resolves cleanly: **experiments = PoC, `apps/watchtower` = the artifact.**
